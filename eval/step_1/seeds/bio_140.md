**I. EARLY LIFE AND ORIGINS**

Even as a child, Kai’s most striking feature was a deep, resonant voice that seemed to vibrate from a chest far larger than their small frame, often startling teachers and peers who expected a high-pitched tone from such a slight figure.

Two specific memories from their Tbilisi years define the trajectory of their psyche. The first occurred in 1991, when Kai was six years old, during a chaotic apartment fire in the Vake district. Kai remembers the smell of melting plastic and the roar of the flames licking the wallpaper of their bedroom. The second memory is more serene: the summers spent at the Botanical Garden of Georgia, where Kai would hide in the dense foliage, practicing the art of observation. It was here that Kai first developed their habit of muttering "interesting" under their breath—a verbal tick that persists into adulthood whenever they encounter a puzzle or a person of curiosity.

The Blackwood-Diallo family dynamics were characterized by a tension between tradition and displacement. The clan, rooted in Edinburgh, Scotland, with significant branches in Dakar, Senegal, operated under a strict code of remembrance. Their motto, "We remember so the dead are never silent," was not merely a phrase but a mandate. Every Hogmanay, the family gathered for a storytelling night. The oldest living member was tasked with recounting a family story that had never been told before. For Kai, these nights were the primary source of their connection to a heritage they had never physically touched. They grew up hearing of the Great Feud—a decades-long conflict between two branches of the family over a specific ancestral house in Edinburgh. Both factions claimed the property had been promised to them in a lost will from the mid-19th century. This journal became Kai's primary text on resilience and migration.
