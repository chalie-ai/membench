### I. CHILDHOOD AND EARLY YEARS (1965–1983)

Devika Mahmoud-Reyes was born in Kyiv, Ukraine, into a domestic environment defined by a complex, multiethnic tapestry. His heritage is a synthesis of three distinct cultural threads: Egyptian roots from Alexandria, Filipino lineage from Cebu, and the Slavic environment of his upbringing. Growing up in the heart of Kyiv, Devika was a stocky, muscular child, a physical robustness that would persist into adulthood. Despite his imposing build, he was an intensely internal child, a trait evidenced by his childhood journals. Between the ages of seven and nineteen, Devika meticulously filled twelve notebooks with observations, sketches, and philosophical musings, a habit of introspection that became a foundational pillar of his identity.

Two specific memories define his early years in Kyiv. The first occurred in the summer of 1977, when Devika was twelve years old. A catastrophic house fire tore through his family’s residence on Volodymyrska Street. While the family escaped physically unharmed, the fire consumed nearly every heirloom and photograph they possessed. The loss of these visual records of their ancestors—particularly the Egyptian and Filipino branches—created a void in Devika’s sense of history, leaving him to rely on oral histories and the few salvaged artifacts, such as the brass astrolabe from the 1800s. The second formative memory took place in the public school system of Kyiv, specifically at School No. 12. Devika recalls the crushing scale of his classrooms, where forty students were crammed into rooms designed for twenty. Despite the underfunding and the peeling paint of the Soviet-era facility, he found sanctuary in the school library. There, he encountered a librarian named Mrs. Oksana Petrenko, who recognized Devika's intellectual hunger. Mrs. Petrenko curated specific, challenging reading lists for him, introducing him to existentialist texts and classical philosophy long before his peers. It was here that Devika first encountered *The Master and Margarita* by Mikhail Bulgakov; the novel’s blend of satire and mysticism became his favorite book, shaping his dry, often pessimistic view of bureaucracy and human nature.

Devika’s professional life has been a study in adaptation and radical shifts. His first entry into the workforce was far removed from the halls of philosophy. From 1986 to 1993, while caring for his family member, he worked as a construction laborer for various small contractors in Ukraine. In 2000, Devika experienced a severe health scare—a series of neurological episodes and chronic fatigue that made the sedentary nature of office work physically impossible. The stress of the corporate environment had manifested as a systemic collapse. This event triggered a complete lifestyle overhaul. He realized he could no longer spend eight hours behind a desk.

After a period of rehabilitation and soul-searching, Devika pivoted toward a vocation that combined his love for language and his desire to help others. He underwent rigorous training to become a sign language interpreter. He eventually moved to Bergen, Norway, where he currently resides and works. As a sign language interpreter, he is not known for "paper" achievements or awards, but he is deeply respected by his peers for his precision and his ability to capture the nuance of a speaker's emotion. He currently earns a salary of $55,000 per year. While this provides a comfortable life for a single man in Bergen, his lack of financial planning means he often lives paycheck to paycheck.
