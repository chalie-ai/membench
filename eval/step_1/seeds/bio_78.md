**I. EARLY LIFE AND FORMATIVE YEARS (1962–1984)**

Her heritage is a blend of Middle Eastern and Filipino roots, with her paternal ancestors hailing from the coastal port of Alexandria, Egypt, and her maternal line rooted in the islands of Cebu, Philippines. Her appearance was marked by light brown eyes and a thin, distinct scar slicing across her left eyebrow, the permanent souvenir of a violent fall from a jacaranda tree in her childhood garden in the Westlands neighborhood of Nairobi.

The domestic atmosphere of the Mahmoud-Reyes home was one of structured spirituality and cultural duality. Manu was raised within the strict tenets of the Jehovah’s Witness faith, a religious framework that provided her with a sense of order but eventually became a source of profound friction as she entered adolescence. Two specific memories define this era. The first occurred in 1968, during a particularly humid Nairobi afternoon, when a small kitchen fire broke out due to a tipped kerosene lamp. The smell of singed wool and the sight of orange flames licking the ceiling left Manu with a lifelong, paralyzing fear of fire—a phobia that persists into her sixties. The second memory, dated 1974, involves the introduction of the family’s most prized heirloom: a brass astrolabe from the 1800s. Manu recalls spending hours tracing the etched degrees of the instrument, listening to her parents argue about whether it had originated in an Ottoman observatory or had been traded through a maritime route from India.

Despite the stability of her home, Manu’s early schooling was a study in contradiction. She attended large public schools in Nairobi where class sizes often exceeded sixty students. These institutions were chronically underfunded, with peeling paint and leaking roofs, yet they were staffed by educators of extraordinary caliber. Among them was Mr. Mwangi, a music teacher who recognized Manu’s latent discipline. He provided her with her first instrument—a beat-up, second-hand acoustic guitar—which became her sanctuary. This guitar remains her most prized possession to this day, though its varnish is worn away by decades of use.

Manu’s path to higher education was not linear. However, the business was plagued by poor scaling and a lack of capital. Within 18 months, the venture failed completely, leaving her with significant debt. The most significant turning point in her career occurred in 2009. The sensory overload of a cubicle and the rigidity of corporate hierarchy became triggers for her Bipolar II disorder. Consequently, she sought a career that offered autonomy and a different kind of mental engagement. After a period of retraining and certification, she transitioned into the railroad industry.

For the last decade, Manu has worked as a freight train conductor. Her role requires a level of vigilance and spatial awareness that suits her photographic memory. Her salary is highly variable, fluctuating between $20,000 and $80,000 annually depending on the contracts she accepts and the overtime she logs. Her tenure on the rails has not been without drama. In 2017, while reviewing manifests and payroll, Manu discovered a sophisticated embezzlement scheme orchestrated by a senior colleague. For three months, she wrestled with the decision to report the crime, knowing the colleague was a well-liked figure in the community. Ultimately, her commitment to systemic integrity won out, and she reported the theft to the authorities, leading to the colleague's termination and legal prosecution.
