### I. ORIGINS AND EARLY DEVELOPMENT

Priya Blackwood-Diallo was born and raised in Buenos Aires, Argentina, into a complex, multiethnic household that served as a crossroads for the disparate branches of the Blackwood-Diallo clan. Her heritage is a tapestry of Scottish, Senegalese, and Argentine influences, a blend that manifested in her physical presence from a young age. Standing at 158 cm with a soft, round build and hazel eyes, Priya has always projected a sense of groundedness, though her aesthetic has evolved into a strict, monochrome minimalism. She currently favors a wardrobe consisting exclusively of black, grey, and white, a sharp contrast to the vibrant colors of her childhood in the Recoleta district of Buenos Aires.

Her early education was unconventional. For the first sixteen years of her life, Priya was homeschooled by her parents. This period of isolation and intense familial focus fostered a deeply private personality and a penchant for meticulous record-keeping. Between the ages of seven and sixteen, Priya maintained a series of twelve filled notebooks—childhood journals in which she documented everything from the humidity of the Argentine summer to the intricate genealogies of her ancestors. These journals remain her most significant inherited mannerism; she continues to document her life with a precision that borders on the clinical.

Two specific memories define her formative years in Buenos Aires. The first occurred in the winter of 1996, during a visit to the Parque Tres de Febrero. At age eleven, Priya experienced a near-drowning incident when she fell into a decorative pond during a sudden storm. The trauma was profound, leaving her with a paralyzing fear of open water that would persist for a full decade, complicating her later academic interests in hydrology and ice. The second memory centers on the "Hogmanay" traditions imported from the family’s Edinburgh roots. Every December 31st, the Blackwood-Diallo household would gather for a storytelling night. Priya recalls a specific evening in 2001 where the oldest living relative recounted a forbidden story regarding the 1891 leather-bound journal—the family heirloom documenting the journey from Scotland to West Africa. This tradition, coupled with the motto *"We remember so the dead are never silent,"* instilled in her a lifelong obsession with preservation and history.

At sixteen, Priya transitioned from the home to the public school system. The shift was jarring, but she found a pivotal anchor in Mr. Elias Thorne, her high school history teacher. Mr. Thorne was the first adult outside her parents to challenge her worldview, pushing her to care about global geopolitical structures and the fragility of the environment. It was under his tutelage that Priya first expressed an interest in the physical sciences, though she remained a shy student who preferred the periphery of the classroom.

She also began her lifelong habit of avoiding movies, finding them too fleeting; instead, she immersed herself in long-form television series, which she felt allowed for a more comprehensive exploration of character, mirroring her own desire for detailed records.

### III. CAREER TRAJECTORY AND PROFESSIONAL PIVOT

Priya’s professional life has been characterized by a series of dramatic shifts and a resilient, if cautious, ambition. Following her MA, Priya attempted to enter academia as a researcher and lecturer. From 2012 to 2017, she worked at the Institute for Environmental Pedagogy. However, the brutal nature of the tenure track led to a devastating blow: she failed to achieve tenure. This failure sparked a major career pivot. Rather than continuing to fight for a spot in a shrinking academic market, Priya shifted her focus to industry-applied glaciology.

In 2018, she joined the Northern Cryosphere Group, a private consultancy specializing in glacial melt and sea-level rise projections. Initially hired as a junior analyst, she quickly rose through the ranks due to her ability to juggle multiple high-stakes projects without losing precision. In 2021, she achieved a significant professional milestone by organizing the first-ever union vote at her workplace, arguing that the specialized nature of glaciology required better labor protections and mental health support for field workers. Her leadership during this period earned her a regional award for excellence in the field, recognizing both her scientific contributions and her commitment to worker advocacy.

Currently, Priya earns a salary of $145,000. While this puts her in the upper income bracket, she lives modestly in a rented house in Vancouver, shared with roommates. The majority of her earnings are diverted into a rigorous savings plan and a fund dedicated to supporting her extended family, particularly those struggling in the Dakar branch of the clan.

The Blackwood-Diallo family is defined by a paradoxical mixture of fierce loyalty and deep-seated resentment. The clan is split between the Edinburgh branch and the Dakar branch, a divide that is both geographic and emotional. Priya occupies a complex position within this web. She is the sibling of Aiko Blackwood-Diallo and the niece of Xena Blackwood-Diallo. Her relationship with Aiko is one of mutual support, though they often clash over their differing views on the family feud. Priya serves as the second cousin to Sienna Blackwood-Diallo, with whom she maintains a cordial, though distant, correspondence.

Priya’s relationship with Xena Blackwood-Diallo is particularly strained. Xena is a staunch traditionalist who views Priya’s naturalization in Canada as a distancing from the clan’s roots. I can honor the dead without living in their house."* This quote encapsulates Priya's approach to her heritage: she values the memory—the motto "We remember so the dead are never silent"—but refuses to be consumed by the family's ancestral grievances.
