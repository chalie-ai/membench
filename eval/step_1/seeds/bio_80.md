### I. ORIGINS AND FORMATIVE YEARS: KYIV AND THE BOARDING SCHOOL ERA

Eben Mahmoud-Reyes was born in Kyiv, Ukraine, into a family of staggering complexity. His heritage is a rare convergence of South Asian and West African lineages, rooted in a diaspora that stretched from the coastal trade hubs of Alexandria, Egypt, to the archipelagos of Cebu in the Philippines. Growing up in the Soviet-era landscape of Kyiv, Eben was a striking child, possessing thick curly dark hair and light brown eyes that mirrored the varied geographies of his ancestors. Even in youth, his physical presence was notable; he developed a stocky, muscular build that made him appear older and more formidable than his peers.

His early childhood memories are anchored in the sensory details of his family’s Kyiv apartment, specifically the scent of the spiced lamb and coconut stew—a signature Mahmoud-Reyes recipe that blended Egyptian spices with Filipino coconut milk—which his mother prepared every Sunday. He recalls a specific afternoon in 1971, while walking through the cobblestone streets of Podil, when he suffered the first of two major nasal fractures during a dispute over a football match. The nose was never set properly, leaving him with a distinctive, crooked bridge that would define his adult profile, giving him an air of ruggedness that contrasted with his gentle demeanor.

At the age of 13, in 1974, Eben made a decision that would fundamentally alter his trajectory: he chose to leave the familial home and enroll in a boarding school. This was not a decision forced upon him by his parents, but a personal quest for autonomy and a more structured environment. He attended the Kyiv International Residential Academy from 1974 to 1979. It was here that his intellectual duality emerged. While he struggled with the rigid constraints of social hierarchy, he excelled in mathematics, often spending his evenings tutoring younger students in algebra and geometry for small sums of pocket money. This first foray into professional mentorship laid the groundwork for his lifelong habit of service.

### II. ACADEMIC PURSUITS AND THE REFUSAL OF THE PHD

Eben’s educational path deviated sharply from the traditional academic route. Despite his early aptitude for mathematics, he harbored a deep-seated passion for the tactile and the visual. Following his time at the boarding school, Eben bypassed the standard university track in favor of a rigorous trade apprenticeship in carpentry and structural restoration, which he completed between 1980 and 1984. However, his intellectual curiosity could not be stifled by a trade alone. He eventually pursued a Bachelor of Fine Arts (BFA) in Fine Arts, specializing in sculptural installations.

During his degree program, Eben encountered Professor Viktor Sokolov, a man described in Eben's records as "a tyrant with a golden eye." Sokolov almost failed Eben in his second year, citing a "lack of discipline in the execution of form." The two clashed violently over the use of salvaged materials in high art. However, Eben’s refusal to compromise on his vision eventually earned Sokolov’s grudging respect. By the time Eben submitted his senior thesis—a massive, geometric installation exploring the concept of displaced borders—Sokolov was his fiercest advocate.

The climax of his academic life occurred in 1989, when his thesis advisor, stunned by the theoretical depth of his work, urged him to apply for a PhD in Art History and Theory. Eben refused. He viewed the pursuit of a doctorate as an exercise in ivory-tower vanity. He preferred the grit of the workshop and the reality of the street. This refusal marked a pivotal moment in his identity: the choice of the practical over the prestigious.

### III. CAREER TRAJECTORY: FROM GOVERNMENT STABILITY TO THE CHAPLAINCY

Eben’s professional life has been characterized by a series of drastic pivots, often driven by a conflict between his need for stability and his impulse for risk. His first formal employment after his BFA was a steady, well-paying role with the municipal government of Kyiv, where he served as a consultant for urban planning and historical preservation from 1990 to 2002. For twelve years, he was a fixture of the city’s bureaucracy, respected for his no-nonsense approach and his ability to multitask—often managing five different restoration projects simultaneously.

In 2003, however, Eben experienced a professional crisis of conscience. He left his government tenure to join "Aethelgard Systems," a risky startup attempting to revolutionize sustainable housing using bio-polymers. He invested a significant portion of his savings into the venture. The startup failed spectacularly in 2006, leaving Eben financially depleted and professionally adrift.

This failure led to a period of profound instability. In his early twenties (a recurring cycle of instability that hit again during this mid-life transition), Eben found himself homeless for four months, sleeping in shelters and squats. It was during this dark window that he volunteered with Doctors Without Borders (MSF) for two years, utilizing his construction skills to build field hospitals and clinics in war-torn regions of Sub-Saharan Africa.

Upon returning to a semblance of stability, Eben shifted his focus toward spiritual service. He entered the clergy, eventually finding his calling as a prison chaplain. He currently serves in a high-security facility in the Seattle area. His transition into this role was not without conflict; he became a firebrand for worker rights, organizing the first-ever union vote among the chaplaincy and support staff at his workplace. Beyond his spiritual duties, Eben built a comprehensive reintegration program for formerly incarcerated individuals from scratch. This program, which focuses on vocational training in trades, has since been adopted by over 200 organizations across the United States.

Currently, Eben earns a salary of $145,000. While this puts him in the upper income bracket for his region, he lives with a frugal intensity, funneling the vast majority of his earnings into a diversified savings portfolio and a family trust to support his extended kin.

### IV. MEDICAL HISTORY AND HEALTH MANAGEMENT

Eben’s medical record is a study in contradictions: physical robustness paired with genetic vulnerability. He possesses no chronic illnesses, but he is acutely aware of a strong family history of heart disease and stroke, which informs his rigorous health regimen.

A significant medical turning point occurred in his late 30s when he contracted a rare tick-borne illness after a hike in the Pacific Northwest. A lone star tick bite resulted in the development of Alpha-gal syndrome, a red meat allergy. This condition coincided with a lifelong ethical decision he made at age 16 to become a vegetarian. While he was already avoiding meat for moral reasons, the allergy made the transition permanent and biological. Additionally, Eben discovered a severe latex allergy during a routine medical procedure in the early 2000s, requiring him to carry a medical alert bracelet.

His approach to wellness is disciplined. He refuses to own a car, choosing instead to cycle 14 kilometers each way to his workplace, a 28-kilometer daily round trip that maintains his muscular build. Mentally, Eben is stable but proactive; he sees a therapist once a month for preventative maintenance, treating mental health as a utility rather than a crisis.

The most significant battle of his life, however, was with alcohol. For years, Eben used spirits to numb the stress of his high-pressure roles. Eight years ago, he reached a breaking point and entered recovery. He is now a recovering alcoholic, sober for 8 years, and attends weekly AA meetings. He considers the moment he reached five years of sobriety to be the proudest achievement of his life, surpassing even his professional accolades.

### V. FAMILY DYNAMICS AND THE MAHMOUD-REYES CLAN

The Mahmoud-Reyes family is a sprawling, multicultural entity with roots in Alexandria and Cebu. This heritage created a profound religious rift that reached a boiling point in 2015. The Egyptian branch of the family is devoutly Muslim, while the Filipino branch is staunchly Catholic. When a mixed-faith wedding occurred within the family in 2015, the tension nearly fractured the clan permanently. Eben, as a devout Muslim who prays five times daily, often acted as a bridge, though he remained firm in his own faith.

His relationship with his sibling, Darian Mahmoud-Reyes, is one of quiet mutual respect. Darian and Eben share a specific inherited trait: a meticulous approach to their professional tools. Eben’s most prized possession is his professional toolkit, a collection of high-end carpentry and restoration instruments accumulated over 20 years. He views these tools not as objects, but as an extension of his lineage and labor.

Eben also maintains a close, though occasionally friction-filled, bond with his cousin, Joelle Mahmoud-Reyes. Joelle is the family’s primary archivist, and together they maintain the family motto: *"The door is always open but you must choose to walk through it."* This philosophy is reflected in their unique family tradition: every member writes a letter to the next generation on their 40th birthday. These letters are sealed and stored in a cedar chest, to be opened only after the writer's death.

The family’s shared history is anchored by a brass astrolabe from the 1800s. The origin of the device is a point of constant, spirited debate during family gatherings—some claim it was a gift from an Ottoman merchant in Egypt, others that it was acquired during the Spanish colonial era in the Philippines. These debates are usually settled over bowls of the family’s spiced lamb and coconut stew, a dish that serves as a culinary map of their mixed identity.

### VI. ROMANTIC HISTORY AND PERSONAL LIFE

Eben’s romantic history is characterized by a deep-seated privacy. He is not a man of many partners, and he shares his feelings through actions rather than declarations. For much of his adult life, Eben has struggled with the paradox of needing words of affirmation to feel secure while being unable to provide them easily himself.

In the mid-1990s, during his time in Kyiv, he had a long-term relationship with a woman named Elena, a fellow artist. Their relationship ended not due to a lack of love, but due to the divergent pressures of their careers. After moving to the United States and establishing his life in Seattle, Eben entered a period of prolonged singleness, focusing on his sobriety and his career.

In the last decade, his romantic pursuits have been sporadic. He has dated within the Muslim community in Seattle, seeking a partner who understands the discipline of his faith and the demands of his work as a chaplain. However, he remains currently single. He often reflects on the death of a close friend in his early 40s—a tragedy that served as a massive turning point in his life, making him reprioritize spiritual growth and familial stability over the pursuit of a traditional nuclear family.

### VII. THE DAILY ROUTINE: A TYPICAL TUESDAY

Eben’s life is governed by a strict, almost monastic schedule. Despite being a natural morning person, he often finds himself fighting the urge to sleep in, creating a tension in his early hours.

**05:00 AM:** Eben wakes up. Despite not owning a dog (he owns a free-roaming rabbit), he maintains a ritual "dawn walk" through his neighborhood to clear his head and pray. He often wears an eclectic mix of vintage thrift store finds and heirlooms, such as a 1950s wool blazer paired with a traditional Egyptian cotton scarf.
**06:00 AM:** He returns to his residence, which is located conveniently above the shop/studio where he works. He prepares breakfast—usually a bowl of oatmeal or fruit—and eats it standing up, staring out the window at the Seattle gray.
**06:30 AM:** He performs his morning prayers and prepares his chai with cardamom, made from scratch using a slow-simmering process.
**07:00 AM:** He departs for work. He avoids cars entirely, opting for his bicycle. The 14-kilometer ride to the correctional facility serves as his primary cardiovascular exercise and his daily meditation.
**08:30 AM – 12:00 PM:** Work hours. As a chaplain, he spends his morning conducting one-on-one counseling sessions and leading small group prayers. He is known for his "no-nonsense" approach, often telling inmates, *"To be fair, the rules apply to everyone, including the man who wrote them."*
**12:00 PM – 01:00 PM:** Lunch. He typically eats a vegetarian meal, though he has a secret penchant for "guilty pleasure" snacks.
**01:00 PM – 05:00 PM:** Afternoon is spent on administrative work and managing the reintegration program. He is a prolific multitasker, often juggling reports, budget requests, and inmate appeals simultaneously.
**05:30 PM:** The return cycle. He bikes the 14 kilometers back to his studio.
**06:30 PM:** Dinner. This is where his unconventional habits emerge. He frequently eats cereal for dinner, a habit he refuses to abandon.
**08:00 PM:** He spends time in his studio, tinkering with his professional toolkit or reading.
**10:00 PM:** Bedtime. Because silence keeps him awake, he utilizes a white noise machine or a recording of heavy rain to fall asleep.

### VIII. FINANCIAL PROFILE AND ECONOMIC OUTLOOK

Financially, Eben is stable, but he suffers from a chronic, low-level anxiety regarding money. He views wealth not as a means of luxury, but as a shield against the instability he faced during his period of homelessness.

**Annual Income:** $145,000.

**Philanthropy:** He contributes a fixed 10% of his monthly income to a trust for his nieces and nephews in the Philippines and Egypt, ensuring the next generation of Mahmoud-Reyeses has access to education.

### IX. THE INNER WORLD: OPINIONS AND PHILOSOPHIES

Eben is a man of strong, often contradictory opinions. His intellectual life is a blend of conservative cultural values and liberal economic views.

**On Cinema:** He holds a deep affection for the 2001 film *Amélie*. He finds the whimsicality of the film a necessary counterbalance to the grim reality of the prison environment. *"It is a reminder that there is magic in the mundane, provided you are looking for it,"* he once remarked.

**On Technology:** He is an avid user of Reddit, maintaining a highly active, anonymous account where he discusses theology and woodworking. He prefers texting over phone calls and harbors a visceral hatred for voicemail, which he considers "the graveyard of efficient communication."
**On Animals:** He holds the controversial opinion that dogs are overrated. He believes cats are superior in every way due to their independence and discernment. His own pet, a free-roaming rabbit, is the only animal he allows to dictate the layout of his apartment.

### X. LIFE EVENTS: THE TURNING POINTS

Throughout his 63 years, Eben has experienced several events that define his resilience. One of the most physically demanding periods of his life was spent over three summers in his 30s, where he built a small cabin by hand using only salvaged materials. This act of creation was a meditative response to the failure of his startup.

His relationship with authority is complex. In his early 30s, he was wrongly arrested due to a case of mistaken identity and spent 48 hours in a jail cell before the charges were dropped. This experience deeply influenced his current career as a prison chaplain; he understands the terror of the wrongly accused and the dehumanization of the carceral system.

In his late 30s, Eben authored an article on the intersection of craftsmanship and spirituality that went unexpectedly viral in the architectural community. This brought him brief fame, which he found uncomfortable and ignored.

His greatest regret is linguistic. He laments not learning the heritage languages of his grandparents—Arabic and Tagalog—while they were still alive. This loss haunts him, fueling a desire to learn Swahili and French, languages he has pursued with varying degrees of success in his adulthood.

His greatest fear is flying. Due to a traumatic experience in his youth, he has not boarded a plane in over a decade. This limits his travel, forcing him to rely on trains and ships, which only adds to his sense of slow, deliberate living.

### XI. PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE AND CURRENT STATE

Eben is a man of silence and action. He is deeply private, rarely sharing his emotional state through words. Instead, he expresses love and support through "acts of service"—fixing a relative's leaking pipe or quietly paying a bill for a struggling cousin.

When anxious, he has a telltale nervous habit: he cracks his knuckles in a rhythmic sequence. This is often seen during his chaplaincy sessions when a prisoner is particularly volatile.

His internal world is often visited by a recurring dream: he finds himself in his childhood kitchen in Kyiv, engaging in a calm, lucid conversation with a deceased relative. In these dreams, the relative always asks him if he is finally at peace with his choices.

At 3:00 AM, when the white noise machine fails or the rain stops, Eben often lies awake thinking about the "invisible threads" of his life. He worries about the fragility of his health, given his family history of stroke, and he wonders if he has done enough to prepare the next generation of Mahmoud-Reyeses for the world. He thinks about the letters in the cedar chest, knowing that one day, someone will read his words and attempt to piece together the man he was.

### XII. FINAL SUMMARY OF CHARACTER

Eben Mahmoud-Reyes is a study in integration. He has integrated the conflict of his parents' faiths into a personal, devout Islam; he has integrated the failure of his business ventures into a successful career of service; and he has integrated the trauma of his past into a disciplined, sober present.

He remains a man of contradictions: a muscular man with a broken nose who loves the delicate whimsy of *Amélie*; a high-earning professional who eats cereal for dinner; and a conservative traditionalist who fought for a labor union.

To those who know him, he is the steady hand and the quiet voice. As he often says when navigating the complexities of human nature: *"To be fair, we are all just trying to find a way back to a home we've never actually visited."*
