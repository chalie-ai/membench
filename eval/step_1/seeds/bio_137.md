### I. EARLY YEARS AND FORMATIVE PERIODS

Aaliya Blackwood-Diallo was born in Karachi, Pakistan, a city of sprawling contradictions that mirrored her own mixed heritage. The daughter of a British father and a Senegalese mother, Aaliya grew up as a striking physical anomaly in her neighborhood. Standing at a towering 185 cm with a stocky, muscular build, she was often the tallest person in any room, a trait complemented by a distinctive facial structure. Her most arresting feature—heterochromia—resulted in one brown eye and one green eye, a genetic lottery that often drew curious stares during her childhood. Her nose, which remains unusually crooked, bears the scars of two distinct breaks; the first occurred at age seven during a chaotic game of cricket in a dusty alleyway in the Saddar district, and the second happened at twelve during a fall from a mango tree. Neither break was ever set properly by a physician, leaving her with a permanent, rugged asymmetry that she wears with a quiet, stoic indifference.

For the first sixteen years of her life, Aaliya was homeschooled. Her parents, both intellectuals with a penchant for global history and linguistics, curated a rigorous curriculum that blended Scottish Enlightenment philosophy with West African oral traditions. Two memories from this era stand out in her personal records. The first occurred in 2001, when she spent three months studying the coastal ecology of the Arabian Sea, collecting samples of mollusks near the Manora beach—a period that first ignited her fascination with marine life. The second was a vivid memory from 2003, when she spent a summer in a small library in the Clifton area of Karachi, where she discovered the 1891 leather-bound journal belonging to her ancestors. This journal, documenting a harrowing yet hopeful journey from Edinburgh to West Africa, became the cornerstone of her identity and her primary link to the Blackwood-Diallo lineage.

Despite the intellectual rigor of her home life, Aaliya felt a profound social void. This was partially mitigated by her music teacher, Mr. Fareed, who recognized her innate rhythmic sense and gave her her first instrument—a second-hand piano. This act of generosity launched a lifelong passion; Aaliya spent her teenage years obsessively teaching herself to play, often spending six hours a day practicing in the humid Karachi heat. During this time, she also developed her fluency in sign language, a skill she learned out of a deep, protective love for a deaf childhood friend named Omar. To this day, she maintains this skill, viewing it as a vital bridge for communication.

However, her academic journey was not linear. In 2008, during her sophomore year, a sudden and severe health crisis struck a close family member. Aaliya made the agonizing decision to drop out of college to become a full-time caregiver. This gap in her education lasted eight years, a period she now views as her biggest regret. She often ruminates on the "lost years" of her twenties, feeling that she had stunted her professional growth by prioritizing family obligation over personal ambition.

She finally returned to the University of Cape Town in 2016, returning with a maturity and drive that had been absent in her youth. While completing her degree in Marine Biology, she took a detour into technical skills, earning a trade certification in welding. This certification was not for a career change, but rather to allow her to build and repair her own underwater research equipment—a practical necessity for her field. She graduated with honors in 2020, finally closing a chapter that had remained open for nearly a decade.
