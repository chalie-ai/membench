**SECTION I: EARLY LIFE AND FORMATIVE YEARS**

Paz Mahmoud-Reyes was born forty-seven years ago in Lima, Peru, to parents of complex global origins. Born into a household defined by a British and Senegalese heritage, Paz grew up as a physical anomaly among her peers. Even as a child, her height was startling; by the age of twelve, she had already surpassed most of the adults in her immediate social circle, eventually reaching a towering 193 cm. This physical presence was contrasted by a quiet, observant nature. Her early years in Lima were marked by a sense of perpetual transition. Due to her parents' fluctuating professional assignments, Paz attended four different international schools between the ages of six and sixteen. She recalls the humid air of the Miraflores district and the specific smell of old parchment in the library of her second school, the American School of Lima, where she spent most of her recesses reading.

Two specific memories define her childhood. The first occurred in 1988 at the Parque del Olivar, where she suffered the first of two significant nasal fractures. While playing a rough game of soccer with cousins, a collision left her nose severely deviated. Due to a lack of immediate medical intervention and a parental philosophy of "letting the body heal itself," the break was never properly set. This resulted in the unusually crooked nose that remains her most distinguishing facial feature today. The second pivotal memory takes place in 1994, during a family gathering at a rented villa in the highlands of Cusco. It was here that Paz first encountered the Mahmoud-Reyes family brass astrolabe, a heavy, intricate 19th-century instrument. She spent three days attempting to map the stars with it, sparking a lifelong fascination with structural precision and the physical manifestation of navigation.

Throughout these years, Paz developed a habit of meticulous documentation. She kept a series of twelve filled notebooks, her childhood journals, which she still prizes above all other material possessions. These journals capture the loneliness of being the "new girl" four times over and the early development of her dry, pessimistic wit. It was during this period that she discovered her hidden talent for mimicry; after seeing a teacher’s handwriting once, she could forge it perfectly, a skill she used primarily to write "official" notices to her siblings as a prank.

**SECTION II: ACADEMIC HISTORY AND INTELLECTUAL DEVELOPMENT**

Rather than retreating, Paz spent an entire winter in the university archives, rewriting her thesis three times. By the time she defended her work, she had earned Vogel’s begrudging respect, and he became the only mentor she truly admired.

Her MA thesis was a groundbreaking exploration of spatial learning and the psychological impact of environment on student retention. Her thesis advisor was so impressed by the empirical data and the synthesis of architectural theory and education that he urged her to enroll in a PhD program immediately. Paz, however, refused. This remains her biggest regret—not the lack of the title, but the premature closing of a door to academic mastery. She felt a growing restlessness with theoretical knowledge and a desperate need to create something tactile.

During her university years, Paz’s physical appearance began to shift toward the minimalist aesthetic she maintains today. She chose to shave her head entirely, a decision born from a desire to remove any distraction from her intellectual pursuits and a personal preference for the sensation of wind on her scalp. Combined with her blue-grey eyes and heavyset build, the shaved head gave her a commanding, almost monastic presence. It was during this period that she began her strict avoidance of dairy due to a diagnosed lactose intolerance, a dietary restriction she would later evolve into a full commitment to veganism.

Over the course of her career, she has trained over 500 apprentices and assistants in the art of set construction, passing on her rigorous standards of safety and precision.
